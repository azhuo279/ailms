# Strategic UX Research for AI Native Logistics Management

## Framing the opportunity

This report interprets **LMS** as **logistics management software and systems**: the layer that coordinates order intake, transportation planning, carrier tendering, warehouse and fulfillment handoffs, in-transit visibility, proof of delivery, settlement, and compliance. The category is not a single clean market; analysts slice it differently into logistics management software, logistics software, transportation management systems, and the broader digital logistics stack. That matters strategically, because the most attractive product space is no longer just ŌĆ£system of recordŌĆØ transportation management. It is increasingly the **decision-and-execution layer** that sits across fragmented order, warehouse, carrier, and compliance workflows. The clearest design implication is that buyers do not mainly need another dashboard. They need a product that reduces coordination overhead in high-friction moments: order changes, consolidation decisions, tender failures, dock bottlenecks, tracking gaps, ETA uncertainty, document chase, invoice discrepancies, and regulatory filings. Vendor materials and user-review evidence converge on the same pain pattern: planning and execution are digitized unevenly, but exception handling is still full of manual work. Oracle still documents manual tendering in common edge cases; Descartes says shippers still face manual follow-ups, tracking drops, uncertain arrival events, and missing documentation; SAP, Blue Yonder, and G2 reviews point to steep configuration, learning curve, and performance friction; and Kuebix users still report unreliable tracking and manual invoice/approval work. For a product team, the opportunity is therefore not to replace every incumbent module. It is to build an **AI-native coordination surface** that can sit on top of fragmented systems, normalize data, identify risk, propose and execute next-best actions under policy, and keep humans in control for high-consequence decisions. That product direction is well aligned with where the market is moving: AI and sustainability are the dominant logistics themes in DHLŌĆÖs latest trend radar, transportation remains the largest functional component inside digital logistics, and cloud deployment already accounts for more than 75% of digital logistics revenue. ## Core user personas

These personas are synthetic composites based on BLS and O*NET occupational definitions, logistics workflow documentation, and observed pain patterns in current systems. **Global logistics director at a multinational shipper.** This user is typically director or senior-manager level, works in manufacturing, wholesale, transportation/warehousing, or large retail, and usually has several years of prior operational experience before stepping into network, budget, supplier, and compliance accountability. BLS notes that transportation, storage, and distribution managers often work more than 40 hours a week, coordinate across departments, and are under deadline pressure; O*NET lists logistics director and global transportation manager among sample titles in this role family. Their behavioral pattern is to manage with KPIs first, dive into firefighting second, and rely on cross-functional coordination with procurement, finance, warehousing, and customer teams. They tend to value auditable automation, scenario visibility, and the ability to explain why a decision was made. Their recurring pain points map cleanly to needs: siloed execution data creates a need for a unified control view; brittle, highly customized workflows create a need for modular orchestration; rising compliance burden creates a need for traceable automation; and volatile capacity and cost conditions create a need for simulation and policy-driven decision support.
